Understanding Taylor Diagrams: A Comprehensive Educational Guide

Rojan
Feb 8, 2026 • 12 min read

Taylor diagrams are sophisticated yet intuitive visualization tools that have revolutionized how researchers compare model performance across multiple datasets. Originally developed by Karl Taylor in 2001 for climate model evaluation, these diagrams have found applications across atmospheric sciences, oceanography, hydrology, and increasingly in machine learning and deep learning model validation. This comprehensive guide explores the mathematics, interpretation, and practical applications of Taylor diagrams, with special attention to their growing role in modern computational modeling.

What Taylor Diagrams Reveal

At their core, Taylor diagrams provide a concise statistical summary of pattern matching between datasets. They simultaneously display three key metrics: the correlation coefficient between a test dataset and reference data, the root-mean-square difference (RMSD) between them, and the ratio of their standard deviations (or variances). This triple display is what makes Taylor diagrams so efficient—information that would normally require multiple plots or tables can be absorbed at a glance.

Taylor diagrams elegantly compress these three fundamental statistical measures into a single two-dimensional plot, making them remarkably efficient for model intercomparison studies. The reference dataset is typically plotted along the horizontal axis, while test datasets occupy positions determined by their statistical relationship to this reference.

The Three Pillars of Taylor Diagrams

1. Correlation Coefficient (r)

The correlation coefficient measures the linear relationship between patterns in the test dataset and the reference dataset. It ranges from -1 to +1, where +1 indicates perfect positive correlation, 0 indicates no linear relationship, and -1 indicates perfect negative correlation. In the Taylor diagram, correlation is represented by the angular position, with the angle $\theta$ measured from the horizontal axis. Higher correlations (closer to 1) appear at smaller angles, closer to the reference point on the horizontal axis.

2. Standard Deviation (σ)

Standard deviation quantifies the variability or spread in the data. The Taylor diagram represents standard deviation as the radial distance from the origin. The reference dataset's standard deviation is typically normalized to 1.0 and plotted on the horizontal axis. Test datasets with similar variability to the reference will plot at similar radial distances, while those with higher or lower variability will plot farther or closer to the origin, respectively.

3. Root-Mean-Square Difference (RMSD)

The centered root-mean-square difference measures the overall magnitude of error between the test and reference patterns after removing any bias in the mean. This metric is not explicitly plotted as an axis but emerges geometrically from the law of cosines: the distance between any plotted point and the reference point represents the centered RMSD. Smaller distances indicate better agreement between patterns.

The Mathematical Foundation

Understanding the mathematics behind Taylor diagrams helps in both construction and interpretation. Let's walk through the step-by-step calculation process.

Step-by-Step Calculation

Let's denote the reference dataset as R and the test dataset as T, where both contain $n$ data points.

Step 1: Calculate Means

$$\bar{R} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} R_i$$ $$\bar{T} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} T_i$$

Step 2: Calculate Standard Deviations

$$\sigma_r = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (R_i - \bar{R})^2}$$ $$\sigma_t = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (T_i - \bar{T})^2}$$

Step 3: Calculate Correlation Coefficient

$$r = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (R_i - \bar{R})(T_i - \bar{T})}{n \cdot \sigma_r \cdot \sigma_t}$$

Alternatively:

$$r = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} R_i \cdot T_i - n \cdot \bar{R} \cdot \bar{T}}{n \cdot \sigma_r \cdot \sigma_t}$$

Step 4: Calculate Centered RMSD

$$E' = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left[(R_i - \bar{R}) - (T_i - \bar{T})\right]^2}$$

Step 5: Normalize (Optional but Common)

For easier comparison across variables with different units:

$$\text{Normalized } \sigma_t = \frac{\sigma_t}{\sigma_r}$$ $$\text{Normalized } E' = \frac{E'}{\sigma_r}$$

The reference dataset then has $\sigma_r = 1.0$ by definition.

The Law of Cosines Relationship

The elegance of Taylor diagrams comes from the geometric relationship:

$$E'^2 = \sigma_r^2 + \sigma_t^2 - 2 \cdot \sigma_r \cdot \sigma_t \cdot r$$

When normalized with $\sigma_r = 1$:

$$E'^2 = 1 + \sigma_t^2 - 2 \cdot \sigma_t \cdot r$$

This equation means that once you plot a point at coordinates $(\sigma_t \cdot \cos(\theta), \sigma_t \cdot \sin(\theta))$, where $\theta = \arccos(r)$, the distance from that point to the reference automatically represents $E'$.

Constructing the Diagram

Polar Coordinate Plotting

For each test dataset:

  1. Calculate $r$, $\sigma_t$, and $E'$ using the equations above
  2. Convert to polar coordinates: radial distance = $\sigma_t$ (normalized), angle = $\arccos(r)$
  3. Plot the point on polar axes
  4. The reference dataset sits at $(1, 0°)$ on the horizontal axis
  5. Optionally draw contours of constant RMSD as arcs centered on the reference point

Interpreting Taylor Diagrams

Ideal Performance

The perfect model would plot exactly on the reference point, having correlation $r = 1.0$, identical standard deviation ($\sigma_t = \sigma_r$), and zero RMSD.

Reading the Signals

High Correlation, Similar Standard Deviation: Points near the reference indicate models that capture both the pattern and amplitude of variability well. These are your top performers.

High Correlation, Different Standard Deviation: Points along the same angular line but at different radial distances show models that capture spatial or temporal patterns well but either overestimate (farther from origin) or underestimate (closer to origin) the magnitude of variability.

Low Correlation, Any Standard Deviation: Points at large angles from the reference indicate poor pattern agreement, regardless of variability matching. These models fundamentally misrepresent the spatial or temporal structure.

RMSD Contours: Circular arcs centered on the reference point represent lines of constant RMSD. Points on the same arc have equivalent overall error magnitudes, though the breakdown between correlation and variability errors may differ.

Applications in Machine Learning and Deep Learning

Taylor diagrams have found increasingly important applications in evaluating machine learning and deep learning models, particularly in domains involving spatial or temporal predictions.

Why Taylor Diagrams for ML/DL?

Traditional ML metrics like MAE (Mean Absolute Error) or RMSE (Root Mean Square Error) tell you how far predictions are from truth but don't reveal whether the model captures the underlying patterns and variability structure. Taylor diagrams address this gap by showing whether your model:

  • Captures the spatial or temporal correlations in the data (pattern matching)
  • Reproduces the correct magnitude of variability (variance matching)
  • Minimizes overall prediction errors (RMSD)

Common ML/DL Use Cases

Weather and Climate Prediction

Deep learning models like U-Net, ConvLSTM, or Transformers that predict weather variables can be evaluated using Taylor diagrams to compare predictions against reanalysis data or observations. Multiple forecast lead times can be plotted simultaneously to show how prediction quality degrades over time.

Image Super-Resolution

When enhancing low-resolution images, Taylor diagrams can compare the reconstructed high-resolution images against ground truth, evaluating whether the model captures both fine-scale texture patterns and overall intensity distributions.

Spatial Data Prediction

Models predicting quantities like air quality, soil moisture, or sea surface temperature across geographic regions benefit from Taylor diagram evaluation. You can compare CNN-based models, Random Forests, Gaussian Processes, and other methods on a single diagram.

Time Series Forecasting

For multivariate time series (like sensor networks or financial data), Taylor diagrams can evaluate how well LSTM, GRU, or Transformer models capture temporal patterns and variability across multiple prediction horizons.

Generative Models

GANs or diffusion models generating spatial patterns (like satellite imagery or climate fields) can be evaluated against real data samples using Taylor diagrams to ensure generated samples have realistic correlation structures and variability.

Example: Evaluating a CNN for Precipitation Prediction

Suppose you've trained a convolutional neural network to predict daily precipitation fields:

  1. Prepare Data: Flatten your predicted precipitation maps and observed precipitation maps into vectors (ensuring spatial alignment)
  2. Calculate Statistics: For each test day or averaging window, compute correlation between predicted and observed fields, compute standard deviation of predicted field (normalized by observed), and compute centered RMSD
  3. Plot Multiple Scenarios: You might plot different model architectures (ResNet, U-Net, etc.), different training dataset sizes, different forecast lead times (1-day, 3-day, 7-day ahead), and different seasons or geographic regions
  4. Interpret Results: Models closer to the reference point better capture both precipitation patterns and intensity distributions. You might find that one architecture excels at pattern correlation but overestimates variability, while another has balanced performance

Comparing Traditional ML vs Deep Learning

Taylor diagrams excel at comparing fundamentally different model types. You might plot:

  • Linear regression baseline
  • Random Forest
  • Support Vector Regression
  • Shallow neural network
  • Deep CNN
  • Recurrent architectures (LSTM/GRU)
  • Attention-based models (Transformers)

This allows immediate visual comparison: Does the added complexity of deep learning actually improve pattern matching and variability reproduction compared to simpler methods?

Practical Implementation Tips

Data Requirements

  • All datasets must be on the same spatial/temporal grid
  • Sufficient sample size (typically $n > 30$) for stable statistics
  • Same variables and units across all datasets being compared

Common Enhancements

  • Color-code points by model type, region, or variable
  • Add symbol shapes to represent different categories
  • Include percent bias as point size or annotation
  • Plot skill scores or additional metrics alongside
  • Use semi-transparent markers when many points overlap

Software Tools

Multiple scientific computing environments provide Taylor diagram functionality:

  • Python: skill_metrics package, custom matplotlib implementations
  • R: plotrix package with taylor.diagram() function
  • MATLAB: Built-in or custom functions
  • NCL (NCAR Command Language): Extensive Taylor diagram capabilities

Advanced Considerations

Multiple Reference Datasets

Sometimes you may want to compare models against multiple observational datasets or reanalyses. You can either create separate diagrams or use different reference points with different RMSD contour centers.

Weighted Statistics

When data points have different reliability or importance, weighted correlation and standard deviation calculations ensure more relevant regions contribute more to the assessment.

Seasonal or Regional Analysis

Creating separate Taylor diagrams for different seasons, regions, or regimes reveals whether model performance varies systematically across conditions.

Ensemble Predictions

For ensemble forecasts or ensemble ML models, you can plot ensemble mean performance and potentially show ensemble spread using error bars or symbol sizing.

Key Takeaways

Taylor diagrams transform complex multi-metric model evaluation into an intuitive visual format. Their power lies in simultaneous representation of pattern correlation, variability matching, and overall error. For machine learning practitioners, they provide crucial insights beyond simple error metrics, revealing whether models capture the underlying structure and dynamics of the system being modeled.

Whether validating climate models against decades of observations, comparing deep learning architectures for spatial prediction, or evaluating ensemble forecasts, Taylor diagrams offer a rigorous yet accessible framework for performance assessment. As Karl Taylor demonstrated over two decades ago, sometimes the best way to understand model behavior is to look at the complete statistical picture—all at once.
